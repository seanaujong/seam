# Explore outside first — a story

> **At a glance.** A field note behind one of Seam's bets — [*explore before you
> specify*](../seam.md#the-bets), and specifically the claim that *premature conformance is
> premature commitment*. The lesson: when you're still figuring out what you want, distance
> from the existing system is a feature, not waste. Don't streamline the exploration away.

## What happened

Someone gave a talk about how he'd built a feature with an AI agent. He isn't a designer —
by his own account, he doesn't have a strong instinct for what "feels good." So instead of
trying to design in his head, he walked through version after version of the idea with the
agent: generate one, look at it, react, ask for another. He recognized the right one when he
saw it, which is exactly how taste works when you can't specify it up front.

The part that made some people uncomfortable: he did all of this **outside the company's
walls.** He stood the prototypes up on a throwaway host, well outside the monorepo and its
conventions, and he deliberately did *not* try to conform to the in-house design system while
he explored. He took inspiration from the outside world to find what he actually wanted —
*before* turning to think about the thing that already existed inside.

## The instinct to "streamline" it

Afterward, some managers asked the reasonable-sounding question: could we *consolidate* or
*streamline* this — have people think through the company's standards from the start, so the
exploration happens inside the lines and there's less to reconcile later?

That instinct is wrong, and it's wrong in a way Seam predicts.

## Why the distance was the point

Conforming from the start feels efficient, but it's premature commitment wearing the costume
of rigor. The existing system is a powerful anchor: the moment the agent (or the person) is
thinking about the current design system, the current components, the current way things are
done, it stops asking "what would actually be good here?" and starts asking "what fits what
we already have?" Those are different questions, and the second one quietly forecloses the
better answer before it's even considered.

Running the agent *outside* the monorepo isn't sloppiness or a detour — it removes the anchor
on purpose, so exploration is about what feels good rather than what conforms. The
"inefficiency" of working outside the walls **is the value**: it's how you find a version
worth wanting. Conformance is real work, but it's the *next* step — you reconcile to the
house style once you have something worth reconciling, deliberately, not before.

So the way you make an existing thing better is, paradoxically, by looking away from it
first. Streamlining the outside-exploration out of the workflow optimizes away the exact step
that produces improvement.

## The general lesson

- **Premature conformance is premature commitment.** Standards, conventions, and the
  surrounding system are constraints to honor *after* discovery, not anchors to design from. The
  tell: if you catch yourself asking *"what fits what we already have?"* instead of *"what would
  actually be good here?"*, you've conformed too early.
- **Distance is a tool.** Deliberately exploring away from the existing thing — a separate
  sandbox, a fresh context that doesn't know the conventions — is how you avoid anchoring.
- **Don't streamline away the divergence.** The step that feels like wasted, unconsolidated
  effort is often the one doing the real work. Resist consolidating it into "just follow the
  standard from the start."
- **Then conform, on purpose.** Once you've found what you want, reconciling it to the
  existing system is ordinary, valuable work — done as a choice, with something worth keeping.
